# The Three Departments and Six Ministries

The 三省六部 (Three Departments and Six Ministries) is a widely used shorthand for a major imperial Chinese administrative model, especially associated with the Sui and Tang and adapted in later dynasties. It is useful here because it makes a governance problem visible: policy formation, scrutiny, administrative coordination, and specialized work should not collapse into one unchecked act.

> This page is an accessible institutional model, not a claim that every dynasty used the same offices with unchanged authority. Names, rank, reporting lines, and practical power varied considerably over time.

## The three departments: a chain of differentiation

| Institution | Conventional high-level function | Governance logic |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 中书省 (Secretariat) | Drafted and formulated imperial policy documents and orders | The proposal should be made explicit before it is carried out |
| 门下省 (Chancellery) | Deliberated, reviewed, remonstrated, and could return problematic drafts through 封驳 | A proposal needs an independent opportunity for objection and correction |
| 尚书省 (Department of State Affairs) | Coordinated administration and supervised the six ministries in the classic model | An approved decision still needs accountable execution and coordination |

The point is not a mechanically linear bureaucracy. Actual statecraft involved the emperor, court deliberation, secretarial offices, censorial institutions, military structures, local government, and changing dynastic arrangements. The enduring analytical value of the three-department model is its differentiation of drafting, review, and execution.

## The six ministries: specialized administrative domains

In the conventional model, the six ministries were under the 尚书省. Their responsibilities also changed by period, but the following summary is a reliable starting point.

| Ministry | Core historical remit | Typical concerns |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 吏部 (Personnel) | Official appointments, evaluation, promotion, and administrative personnel procedures | Who is qualified, appointed, responsible, or reassigned |
| 户部 (Revenue) | Households, census, land and tax administration, state finance and grain | Population, revenue, fiscal capacity, and resources |
| 礼部 (Rites) | State ritual, education, examinations, diplomatic and tributary reception | Legitimacy, ceremony, learning, examination, and formal relations |
| 兵部 (War) | Military administration, officer personnel, logistics, transport, and related records | Mobilization, military administration, coordination, and defense support |
| 刑部 (Punishments) | Penal law, judicial administration, punishments, and case review | Legal standards, procedure, accountability, and sanctions |
| 工部 (Works) | Public works, construction, water control, technical production, and related labor/material administration | Infrastructure, construction, engineering, and material execution |

The six ministries did not monopolize every function named here. For example, judicial work also involved institutions such as the 大理寺 and 御史台, while military command could be organized through other institutions. The value of the table is to show differentiation of administrative responsibility, not to erase those distinctions.

## Why the model remains useful

The model asks four durable questions:

1. Has the proposal been made visible enough to assess?
2. Can someone with a distinct role challenge it before action becomes costly?
3. Who coordinates execution once it is approved?
4. Which specialist responsibility owns each part of the work—and what record remains?
